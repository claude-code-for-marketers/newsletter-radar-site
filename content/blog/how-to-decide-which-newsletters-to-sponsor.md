# How to Decide Which Newsletters to Sponsor

Newsletter sponsorships are easy to buy and hard to judge.

Most advertisers start with the same inputs: subscriber count, open rate, audience description, and a media kit. Those numbers are useful, but they are also seller-reported. They tell you how a newsletter describes itself. They do not tell you whether companies like yours keep spending there.

That is the question that matters.

If you are deciding where to sponsor, do not start with "which newsletter is biggest?" Start with a better question:

> Do advertisers like us keep coming back here?

That shift turns newsletter buying from a guess into a placement decision.

## The problem with media-kit buying

A media kit can tell you reach, format, price, and audience positioning. It cannot tell you whether the placement held up after the first test.

Two newsletters can look similar on paper:

- similar subscriber counts
- similar topics
- similar pricing
- similar audience descriptions

But one may have advertisers renewing for months while the other is full of one-and-done tests.

Those are very different buying decisions.

Advertisers do not keep paying for placements that do not work for them. You may not be able to see their conversion rates, but you can often see their behavior. If the same type of company keeps showing up in the same newsletter month after month, that is a stronger signal than a publisher's claim about audience quality.

Subscriber count tells you reach. Retention tells you whether advertisers keep trusting the channel.

## Use three filters: fit, proof, and position

A better newsletter sponsorship decision has three parts.

## 1. Fit: do companies like you advertise here?

First, look at the sponsor mix.

If you sell to developers, a newsletter with repeated devtools, infrastructure, and technical SaaS sponsors is probably more relevant than a general business newsletter with a larger list. If you sell fintech software, you want to know whether finance, compliance, and B2B software companies already show up there.

Fit is not just topic alignment. It is category evidence.

Ask:

- What types of companies sponsor this newsletter?
- Are they similar to us in audience, business model, or stage?
- Are they running once, or do they keep appearing?
- Are direct competitors or adjacent companies present?

The goal is to find newsletters where the sponsor roster suggests the audience already responds to companies like yours.

## 2. Proof: do advertisers keep coming back?

Fit tells you whether the audience might be relevant. Proof tells you whether advertisers keep betting on it.

Look for:

- repeat sponsors
- long-tenure sponsors
- advertisers that increase frequency over time
- category-specific retention, not just overall averages

The category-specific view matters. A newsletter may work well for consumer apps but poorly for B2B SaaS. A blended repeat rate can hide that. What you want to know is whether sponsors like you come back.

This is where retention becomes useful. You are not claiming to know a sponsor's exact ROI. You are reading revealed preference: what they keep doing with their budget.

If several companies in your category have sponsored a newsletter for six or twelve months, that is a meaningful signal. If many tried it once and disappeared, that is a caution sign.

## 3. Position: where are competitors present or absent?

The third filter is competitive position.

A competitor's newsletter footprint can reveal three useful things:

- validated placements you are missing
- placements they tested and abandoned
- whitespace where similar companies advertise but your direct competitors do not

Each one leads to a different action.

If a competitor is active in a newsletter and has been there for months, you may be looking at a validated channel. If they appeared briefly and stopped, that placement may deserve more skepticism. If lookalike companies keep sponsoring a newsletter but your direct competitors are absent, that may be a whitespace opportunity.

The point is not to blindly copy competitors. The point is to stop making the decision without knowing where the market is already spending.

## A simple evaluation checklist

Before buying a sponsorship, answer these questions:

1. Which companies have sponsored this newsletter recently?
2. How many are in our category or sell to a similar buyer?
3. Which sponsors have repeated or stayed active over time?
4. Which sponsors appear to have tested once and stopped?
5. Are our competitors present?
6. Are there lookalike advertisers with long tenure?
7. Does the price make sense given the strength of those signals?

If you cannot answer those questions, you are buying from the media kit alone.

That may still be fine for a small test. But if newsletter sponsorships are becoming a real channel, you need a better planning layer.

## The better buying question

The old question is:

> How big is this newsletter?

The better question is:

> Do advertisers like us keep coming back here?

That one question changes the decision. It pushes you toward fit, proof, and competitive context instead of surface-level reach.
